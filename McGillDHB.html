Visualizing

Housing Data

Overview

Client: McGill School of Architecture 
Project type: Data visualization website 
Role: UX/UI Designer
Team: 2 UX/UI Designers, 1 design lead, 1 homepage art director, 2 PMs, multiple engineers
Timeline: 4 months ~ 2022

McGill School of Architecture teamed up with us to build a website for key players in the housing market. How we designed complexity would determine whether these vastly different industries could align to foster a sustainable housing future.

In collaboration with the team, I worked on several pages and played a pivotal role in building out the design system, as well as prototyping for testing. As a result, the final prototype was met with unanimously positive feedback.

context

Ground-breaking progress cannot be made without common ground

The problem and objective

For Canadians to build more sustainable housing, homebuilders, financiers and policymakers required a shared understanding of housing data. The problem was, each industry had their own way of communicating so essential knowledge was scattered, lacked standardization and was inaccessible to outsiders.

That’s where we came in to research, strategize and design a web experience that would serve as a homebase of information for all three industries.

Challenges

Small budget

Complex academic subject matter

Niche subject matter meant few websites to reference

Discover

I don’t have to see the whole staircase to take the first step

Making sense of the chaos

I was the last designer to be onboarded to a project which was already in phase 2. Trying to catch-up was initially overwhelming, but instead of getting stuck on the details, I thought about the big picture goals each user group had.

Using the whitepapers, and findings gathered in a focus group conducted by our UX lead, I created personas that would serve as my north star when designing.

Define

Design is more than decoration, it’s communication

With user goals and pain points in mind, I decided on three guiding principles.

Design principles

Make complex data easier to understand, compare, analyze and share

Ensure features relevant to each user group have high discoverability

Modernize the UI to set it apart from other archaic academic websites

Develop & Deliver

Bridging the knowledge gap with design

We successfully delivered 62 key screens made of 75+ components. However, I’m only going to highlight key pages I worked on.

Phase 1's design failed to communicate what the site has to offer each user group

1. Lack of imagery and dated UI wasn't engaging.
2. Cards were not effective CTAs.
3. Text heavy-design failed to retain users.

Homepage from Phase 1

The new homepage effectively redirects users to the most relevant features for them

1. Modern design, inspired by architectual sketches, engages users with animations and illustrations.
2. Hero, which my colleague Gregory designed, highlights most important feature for all 3 user groups.
3. My animations highlight the most relevant feature for each user group.
4. High discoverability of key features using new UI and active language in CTAs.
5. Encourages users to trust the site by crediting the academics behind it.

Redesigned homepage

Phase 1's case study design didn't match client or user preferences

1. Academics don't like excessive scrolling so this layout was too long.
2. Client felt title blocks were a waste of space.
3. Uses too many pictures, which the client didn't have enough of for every case study.

Case study design from phase 1

The case study redesign makes reading detailed information about building initiatives efficient

1. Sticky navigation bar reduces scrolling and leaves enough negative space for information to breathe.
2. Title blocks are removed and replaced with simple headers.
3. Only needs one image per case study. More images can be added with carousels.
4. Grouping related metrics makes scanning the data easier.

Redesigned case study page

The case study template is used for housing and material passports too because they share similar use cases

Sharing layouts, with minor adjustments, reduces workload for designers and devs, and reduces cognitive load for users; they don't need to learn how to navigate different page layouts.

Housing passport that gives detailed information about a property's sustainability

Material passport that provides information about a material's sustainability

I designed the materials page based on a client mockup

The page allows users to browse building materials before clicking into their respective pages to learn more.

Client mockup of the materials page

The client wanted too many ratings on the cards

Instead of combining the number of hazards into a summative health score, they wanted to display it in detail so users could "decide for themselves what was considered unhealthy". And instead of one summative affordability rating, they wanted two different ratings for new vs. reused materials.

Client preferred card designs vs. my preferred card design

After articulating my design decisions, we thankfully went with my preferred design

- Limiting ratings to 3 per card reduces cognitive load and the time it takes to make a decision
- I decided on the filters based on whitepapers about the typical homebuilding design process
- Users can sort from best to worst for each sustainability rating reducing decision fatigue
- To make sharing data easier, users can select multiple materials and export their sustainability data as a CSV

The glossary explains industry-specific terms that may be hard to understand from a web search  

1. To match users' mental modal, I took inspiration from textbook glossaries.
2. Headers span both columns to reduce scrolling.
3. Words are displayed in the form of dropdowns to make scanning the page easier.
4. With a search bar and filters, users are sure to find what they're looking for.

Visual System

The design system was underdeveloped so I improved it for efficient prototyping

I expanded the capabilities of their existing components, created new ones and reorganized the components into pages according to the atomic design system the agency followed.

Where old meets new

I wanted to update the visual design, but given our budget, the changes had to be simple. To have the site embody the credibility of a respected educational institution, while also setting it apart from the drab textbook look of other academic websites, I updated the icons and changed the header font from Inter to Poppins to match the line illustrations we had created.

Outcomes

100% positive feedback on the UX/UI

We were unable to run usability tests due to budget constraints, but a feedback session where 13 users interacted with the prototype was conducted. Feedback was affinity mapped, and we found while users had concerns about the accuracy of data provided by the McGill team, 100% of participants expressed positive feedback about the user experience and UI design.

What Our Users Had To Say

Educational, exposes concepts on circular economy/material ecology that are normally quite complex to learn about.

Concepts are explained in ways that are accessible to anyone.

The design of the website, and ease of interpretation, are current strengths.

Very visual in terms of results, which makes it easier to understand.

Reflection

Design for user needs, not wants

It’s important to handle client expectations early on to stay within budget. The client was ambitious and wanted a lot of features and functionality, but it was our job to explain why we should only design for user needs rather than wants resulting in the most impactful solution.
