Visualizing

Housing Data

Overview

Client: McGill School of Architecture 
Project type: Data visualization website 
Role: UX/UI Designer
Team: 2 UX/UI Designers, 1 design lead, 1 homepage art director, 2 PMs, multiple engineers
Timeline: 4 months ~ 2022

With robust housing data in hand, McGill School of Architecture teamed up with us to build a website for key players in the housing market. How we designed complexity would determine whether these vastly different industries could align to foster a sustainable housing future.

In collaboration with the team, I worked on several pages and played a pivotal role in building out the design system, as well as prototyping for testing. As a result, the final deliverable was met with unanimously positive feedback.

context

Ground-breaking progress cannot be made without common ground

The problem and objective

For Canadians to build more sustainable housing, homebuilders, financiers and policymakers required a shared understanding of housing data. The problem was, each industry had their own way of communicating so essential knowledge was scattered, lacked standardization and was inaccessible to outsiders.

That’s where we came in to research, strategize and design a web experience that would serve as a homebase of information for all three industries.

Challenges

Small budget

Pages had to be quickly prototyped and iterated for client feedback.

Challenging subject matter

The information was complex and academic in language for both ourselves and users.

Few websites to reference

The subject matter was niche so there were few websites we could reference.

Discover

I don’t have to see the whole staircase to take the first step

Making sense of the chaos

When the client provided us with whitepapers, I was initially overwhelmed. Linoleum floors with a Eutrophication Potential of 45.00 kg Neq? Stucco on CLT structure with a Global Warming Potential of 451.08 kg CO2eq? Ummm... huh? The clock was ticking, I was the last designer to be onboarded to the project, and I was lost.

So instead of getting stuck on the details, I thought about the big picture goals each user group had. Using the whitepapers, and findings gathered in a focus group conducted by our UX lead, I created personas that would serve as my north star when designing.

Define

Design is more than decoration, it’s communication

With user goals and pain points in mind, I decided on three guiding principles.

Design principles

Empower users

to make sustainable decisions for their own building projects by making complex data easier to understand, compare, analyze, and share.

Clearly communicate

which features are most useful for each user group to make wayfinding easier.

Modernize

the UI to differentiate it from other academic websites that tend to look archaic, cluttered and text-heavy.

Site architecture

With our three user groups in mind, we created a suitable site architecture with the aim of directing users to the right features and tools for them. It’s not pretty, but with a tight budget, it got the job done.

Develop & Deliver

Bridging the knowledge gap with design

After rounds of feedback and iteration, and a copy paste marathon to input scientifically correct numbers, we successfully delivered multiple pages. In this case study, I’ll only highlight the ones I collaborated on.

The homepage effectively communicates the website’s purpose and features

The mockup from phase 1 was text-heavy, visually underwhelming and lacked CTAs. In phase 2, we use stunning and interactive animations created by myself and another designer to illustrate the capabilities of each feature. Using CTAs and active language, we redirect primary users to the most useful pages for them. Please turn on your volume for all videos to listen to the rationale.

The housing map enables users to see building data at a city, neighbourhood, and property level

Key decision makers needed a way to efficiently compare and analyze up-to-date housing data, instead of getting lost in spreadsheets, so data is broken down into scales, tables, and graphs, which makes analysis easier. Depending on the filters applied, granularity of the data changes.

The housing passport presents building-specific data about affordability, energy performance, and carbon performance

Using principles of design pertaining to typography, colour, and layout, we designed a housing passport template with a clear hierarchy making the data easy to scan. To avoid walls of text, info icons explain uncommon terminology, and to reduce scrolling, users can navigate to different sections using the sticky navigation bar.

Users can explore building materials and compare their environmental impacts at a glance

Instead of aggregating material data from multiple sources across the web, users can quickly compare materials by the standardized ratings displayed on cards, and filters allow users to find alternatives based on use case and material category. Users can even download a spreadsheet of selected materials with the most relevant metrics to share with their team.

Material passports provide detailed data about a material’s impact on health and the environment

The page structure follows the same format as the housing passport so users don’t need to relearn a new page layout. To help users find related materials, a carousel at the bottom of the page displays items based on related use case and material category. 

The client requested a supplier map, which promotes buying materials locally, but budget constraints prevented us from building it. As a solution, we redirect users to prefilled Google Map searches, which offers better search functionality anyway.

With design scenarios, users can design their own buildings

Builders can design up to 3 buildings simultaneously using a form and compare them side-by-side to understand their impact on global warming. Edit, delete, and add designs easily for an enjoyable and informative iterative process.

The glossary explains industry-specific terms that may be hard to understand from a web search  

The glossary acts as a common point of reference for users from three different industries. The words are displayed in the form of dropdown buttons for effortless page scanning which reduces scrolling and visual overwhelm.

Visual System

Where old meets new

We wanted the website to embody the credibility of a respected educational institution, while also setting it apart from the drab textbook look of other academic websites. Taking inspiration from architectural sites, and architectural blueprints, we went for a modern and simple UI that makes use of clean lines for illustrations.

Outcomes

Nothing but positive feedback

We were unable to run usability tests due to budget constraints, but a feedback session where 13 users interacted with the prototype was conducted. Feedback was affinity mapped, and we found that there were no usability issues, and 100% of participants expressed positive feedback about the user experience and UI design.

What Our Users Had To Say

Educational, exposes concepts on circular economy/material ecology that are normally quite complex to learn about.

Concepts are explained in ways that are accessible to anyone.

The design of the website, and ease of interpretation, are current strengths.

Very visual in terms of results, which makes it easier to understand.

Reflection

Design for user needs, not wants

It’s important to handle client expectations early on to stay within budget. The client was ambitious and wanted a lot of features and functionality, but it was our job to explain why we should only design for user needs rather than wants resulting in the most impactful solution.
